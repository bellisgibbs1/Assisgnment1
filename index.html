Broadway Is Back

Septmeber 30, 2021

Briana Ellis-Gibbs

Isaiah, age nine, and his parents, Joshua and Delicaa, were walking down 8th avenue in front of the New York Times building when Isaiah turned around and described Broadway's reopening in one word.

"Perfect," said Isaiah.

When Broadway shut down in early March 2020 due to the COVID-19 pandemic, only 20% of shows profited off their investment, according to CNBC, while other shows were on the verge of reopening.

Broadway lost revenue due to the pandemic, but Off-Broadway profits will take a harder hit because fewer people are likely to go back to see an Off-Broadway performance.

Number of People that Plan on Returning to Broadway

45% of active theatergoers will immediately return to Broadway

38% of theatergoers plan to see an off-Broadway show

21% of New Yorkers plan to go to Broadway immediately or wait a least a month to return.

Source: Scholl Research.

Off-Broadway shows will depend on over half (56%) of donors who expect to donate to Off-Broadway shows after the pandemic to aid in its comeback.

Source: Scholl Research.

Although many theatergoers are anxiously waiting to return to Broadway, some have reservations.

Even with reservations in mind, many New Yorkers feel nostalgic about Broadway reopening.

Steve Bisgoy, who is 54 years old, who works as a CFO, and Jen Bisgoy, who is 52 years old, a stay-at-home mom, relived their own Broadway memories. They would often come from New Jersey to Broadway to see shows with their children as they grew up. The couple is excited to see their family's favorite show, Phantom of the Opera, once again.

Ages of Potienal Theatregoers

49% of New Yorkers ages 35 to 54 plan immediately return to Broadway.

35% of New Yorkers under 35 expect to return immediately.

33% of theatergoers 55 years old and older will return.

Source: The Scholl Research Center

According to Playbill, Broadway goers like Steve Bisgoy are essential to New York City; they generated $1.83 billion from 2018-2019. 96,900 individual’s jobs depend on theatergoers to pay their bills. Many of those who work on Brodaway lost their health insurance during the panemdic, and had to depend on unemployment checks to pay their bills, or had to take another job. Restaurants, hotels, vendors, and even the MTA depend on the billions of dollars that consumers spend on Broadway to survive.

Lai Mon Ma, who works at a brochure stand outside of Bryant Park with 34th St Partnership, says that the company removed the brochures informing people about Broadway shows, which has drastically hurt her business. According to Lai, the lack of brochures on Broadway shows has slowed down business because many of the brochures’ people wanted were about Broadway and tourist attractions.

The Broadway League found that the Broadway audience in 2019 is 65% tourists.
